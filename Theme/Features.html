Material

The modern paperclip is nearly always made from a complex carbon treated alloy of iron, called steel.
Steel is a material unique for a variety of factors, nearly all of which help to correct the natural errors of more traditional iron and cast iron products.
In its purest form iron is vulnerable to a factor coined in the early 1900's, as "Taylors Dislocation" which represents the ease of atoms within a given material to shift and play.
In modern times, and especially when documenting the factors of more complicated materials, we tend to refer to this maluable flexable trait as ductility.
Ductile metals are metals that can easily be shaped and used by humans, metals with incredibly high "stretch-abillity/flexabillity" are materials like silver, platinum, iron, copper and gold.
Gold is so incredibly ductile that a single gram of gold can be stretched and shaped into a wire nearly 2,300 meters long!
So why you might ask is ductility a weakness of iron based products? And moreover, why would you want to inhibit or lessen that quallity? The answer lies in another quallity called hardness.
hardness is the abillity of a material to resist indentation or abrasion, and is nearly synonomous with the term "strength".
If it helps, picture iron as a limber and agile runner, who can move quickly and adapt to strange and various pressures, trying out for the football team.
while the speed and agility of the individual would likely serve them well, without a viable ammount of strength or resiliance, the runner would likely be outright destroyed.
with this metaphore in hand, steel is a runners thicker, beefier cousin. Far stronger, and slightly slower, Steel preforms overall with admirable grace, and intense strength.
